Lima, Peru

April 2026

Layered Lima

It's Good Friday and big business for palm weavers. Looks like this art form is a lifelong pursuit.

Good Friday was super busy in Lima's historic old town. Las siete iglesias — the seven churches — is a Holy Thursday/Good Friday tradition where pilgrims visit seven churches, representing Jesus's final stops.

Inside we were surprised to see the sculptures covered in purple cloths. This is a European tradition observed the Sunday before Easter through to Easter Sunday — a symbol of grief, building to a dramatic unveiling.

Not covered is a tearful Mary, in her fancy mourning outfit.

Mary is particularly important in Peru, even more so than Jesus. Her sun/moon crown was designed to appeal to indigenous traditions around fertility and Mother Earth. Living Jesus paintings, 64% Catholic in Peru today.

The masks are a subtle critique of the Spanish elite — caricatures that thumb their noses at oppressors under the guise of religious celebration.

A popular subject this week for chalk art.

The soldier on the horse is Francisco Pizarro. With only 168 soldiers, he defeated tens of thousands of Incas in 1532, using horses, swords, and guns to capture Atahualpa and move the capital to Lima. The José de San Martín memorial faces the Pizarro statue — independence 1821.

There are a series of sculptures in the historic centre of Lima representing the "Tapadas Limeñas" — women who wore the saya y manto with only one eye visible for over 300 years. Authorities tried to ban it; women wanted the anonymity and class equality it provided.

This is Jerónimo de Aliaga, a key conquistador alongside Pizarro. He built this house in 1835 and it still stands, continuously occupied by his descendants.

Here we are with Gonzalo de Aliaga, the 17th generation after Jerónimo and current owner. (Our guide told him Jessica was an influencer to score the photo!)

The house was beautiful all the way down to the luggage they used when they travelled from Spain — an intricately carved wooden box. Heavy boxes like this actually helped stabilize ships.

El Bolivarcito, the famous bar at the Gran Hotel Bolivar. The Pisco Sour was invented by Victor Vaughen Morris in the 1910s (not at the Bolivar). The Gran Hotel Bolivar opened in 1924 and bartenders migrated from Morris's Bar. Día Nacional del Pisco Sour = first Saturday of February.

Now here's the sad part…this one was bottom of the barrel. They serve 1,200 Pisco Sours a day using a milkshake blender. Morris would roll over in his grave.

Lima is known as the city of balconies — balcones de cajón, built from the 17th to 19th centuries. Thousands have been lost to earthquakes. They evolved from the mashrabiya tradition, offering anonymity and a natural air conditioning effect.

Crowds at the end of Holy Week make it a perfect time for street performers — balancing acts with metal bars through costumes, and the amazing thumb-holding-pole trick.

We stayed in the Barranco neighbourhood, known for its creative vibe. This is the work of Jade Rivera — a self-taught street artist who explores social realities, inequality, childhood, memory, and emotion.

This is a large-scale Jade Rivera from 2018, depicting two boys in a dreamlike scene — a boy with a book and giant snail shell, and a boy drawing with a heart-backpack and keyhole. The hummingbird represents hope, resilience, and spiritual energy. No key for the lock.

A block away is another of Jade's murals (2019). I wonder if she's holding the key to his younger self's backpack.

From Jade's soft, ethereal work to this bold piece (I love the flowers cascading over the women like fancy hats)…

…to this little bit of toothy aggression, anything and everything goes!

Much to dream about…much to do. Words to live by.

And now to the market! Chicken, fruit, and vegetable aisles — and no waste in the meat department, with tripe, cow's feet, and organs doing booming business.

And then there's this guy having a bit of fun with the Guinea pigs…

A pretty colonial building with a lone black vulture keeping watch over the door. Black vultures catching wind, gliding over the city — rather eerie.

After I had a sit down on a bench with Paddington in Edinburgh…found Paddington at home in Peru. Also spotted: Mafalda — an Argentine comic character by Quino, and Condorito with his dog Washington — a Chilean comic by Pepo.

A flower vendor getting ready for her day.

The Malecón is such a treat in this city. This 10km cliff-top path between Barranco, Miraflores and San Isidro has stunning ocean views.

The beach at Barranco was hopping! Cold southern Pacific waters and lots of people splashing anyway.

No disagreement on the beach that a bit of mud wrestling can't resolve.
