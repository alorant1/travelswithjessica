Cultivated Chinchero

We have left the city of Cusco but we remain in the province of Cusco where we have begun to explore the peaks and valleys of the Andes Mountains. It's a remarkably rich region with everything from beautiful artisan handicrafts to amazing Inca sites giving us a glimpse into the culturally rich and sophisticated society that was the Incan empire. All that in a stunning landscape!

Our first stop was Chinchero where we visited the Centro de Textiles Tradicionales del Cusco. Here we learned about alpaca wool — from shearing, cleaning, and spinning to dyeing and weaving. "Baby alpaca" is a quality grade, not an age.

The white wool is dyed with natural flowers through careful boiling times and colour-fixing processes.

To clean the wool they use grated saqta root — aka Incan shampoo. It contains saponins, which work well on alpaca wool because alpaca fleece is naturally low in oils.

Blue is a special colour using the lupin flower with a longer dye time. The wool is soaked in a fermentation fluid for two weeks and then they add the lupin flowers for another two weeks.

The source of red dye is quite a surprise! It comes from the cochineal insect which is a scale insect that lives on the prickly pear cactus. It looks like a white cottony fuzz on the paddles of the cactus, but underneath is a tiny black insect. When it's dried and crushed, you get a deep red dye.

Our lovely guide, Katia, outside where they do the boiling of the wool, and me sporting my new hat!

Creative drainage along the street in Chinchero.

Outside the weaving centre were many stands with women selling textiles — watch for knitting in progress as proof of handmade goods.

We happened upon a celebration, possibly the birthday of the family's matriarch. The colonial church here was built on top of Inca buildings — the cross at the top features a sun symbol (Inca) and a skull and crossbones at the bottom.

Our first archaeological site on this trip was the Chinchero Archaeological Site — royal estate foundations with terraces, set against the Vilcabamba and Urubamba mountains.

The terraces we see all around the province of Cusco climbing up mountainsides served for farming, drainage, and erosion control — feeding the royal estate and the surrounding region.

A little "Abbey Road" vibe to our walk along the terraces.

Our first peek at the Sacred Valley — the breadbasket of the Incas. Corn was sacred and strategic, grown in this microclimate along the Urubamba River alongside potatoes, quinoa, and beans.

Quinoa growing roadside.

The Salt Pans of Maras — managed by 400–600 families across 3,000–5,000 ponds. Entirely manual and solar-driven.

The natural mountain spring water comes out here at 24% salinity and 32°C. Hand-built channels direct it into the ponds; after 3–4 weeks of evaporation, workers scrape and dry the salt.

This farmer is prepping his ponds for the next flooding — taking tiny steps to pat down the mud walls. Up to eight harvests are possible in the dry season.

Salt bagged and ready to ship. The value chain tells quite a story: $0.25–0.30/kg at the farm gate → $2–6/kg for export → $8–20+/kg at retail as gourmet salt. A 40x–80x increase along the chain.

Our final stop of the day was the Zona Arqueológica de Moray — concentric circles 30–40m deep, with a 15°C temperature range between levels. It served as an open-air agricultural laboratory for potatoes, corn, and quinoa, built 100–150 years before the Spanish conquest of 1532.
