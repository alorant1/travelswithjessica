Cusco, Peru

April 2026

Colonial Cusco

Of course we decided to explore rather than take it easy. The San Blas neighbourhood is an artisan hub full of craftspeople.

It's a pretty neighbourhood with narrow, cobbled streets. Inca stone foundations support everything — you can see the precision of elite stonework versus simpler residential walls.

This fellow was playing his armadillo-backed guitar across the narrow street from the weaver.

This is the square in San Blas, dominated by the colonial church. The Spanish built many of their structures on top of Inca structures.

All that exploring on day one in Cusco led to altitude sickness. Coca leaves and bed rest were the prescription.

This dog seemed to have permanent residency outside our hotel. Dogs are revered in Inca culture as guides for the dead — El Pueblo de Los Perros.

Plaza de Armas, the main square in Cusco, was the capital of the Inca empire. Pizarro arrived in 1533; the cathedral was built on an Inca palace, and the Jesuit church stands beside it.

Every day we were in Cusco, school groups were practising dances for Corpus Christi — Catholic saints paraded through the streets, beautifully blending Inca and Catholic traditions.

I always wondered if cities ever cleaned their fountains…they do! The fountain's history: originally an ushnu pyramid, replaced by a French-designed fountain in 1872 ordered from an American catalog — copies exist in Savannah, Poughkeepsie, and Broadway. An "Apache" statue was added, torn down in 1969, and Manco Capac installed in 2011.

This is the Inca stone style built for elite or sacred structures — flat faces, angular joints, no mortar. Geometry, friction, and weight do the work. This street contains the famous 12-angled stone.

This "puma" is around the corner from the more famous 12-angled stone. In Inca cosmology: puma = life on earth, condor = the gods/heaven, snake = the underworld/dead. Intentional carving or cloud-gazing? You decide.

Another secret to no mortar: tongue and groove joints, or metal keys and cramps — molten copper/bronze alloys poured into grooves between stones.

Another bit of Inca magic was building walls tilting slightly inward from bottom to top. This improves earthquake stability, with gravity reinforcing the joints rather than working against them.

A testament to all this Inca engineering is Coricancha — the most important Inca temple, dedicated to Inti, the sun god. Gold was stripped by the Spanish and the Santo Domingo church was built on top. The 1650 and 1950 earthquakes damaged the church but left Inca stonework standing — renewing appreciation for Inca engineering.

This double-jamb doorway has a very special stone that wraps around the doorway with six faces and five smooth corners — a marker of the most sacred and prestigious buildings.

A trip to San Pedro market in Cusco is a sight to behold — a clothing stall with two old pedal sewing machines, women trying on skirts and dresses right in the aisle.
