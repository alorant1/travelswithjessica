Bring on Bologna

I said it the first time we visited Bologna, and it took two years to come to fruition, but I am back in Bologna to learn Italian!

We had to get a Fist-Day-of-School photo! Who sets to go to a school in a historic building with statues adorning the staircase? We do! We spent three glorious weeks at Academya Lingua and I can’t wait to go back! What a way to learn! The school was amazing, the teachers were fantastic and we came away with way more language skills and confidence than we arrived with.

Our studies were not just in the classroom. Not only is the oldest university in the western world here, Bologna is also home to Carpigiani Gelato University and as such is a bit of a gelato Mecca. It became evident that locals sit firmly on one of two teams: Team Cavour and Team Santo Stefano. This was clearly an invitation for further study. We decided to supplement our language studies with a PhD dissertation on the best gelato in Bologna. Gilly already did one PhD so it was an easy leap into a second one. And this time she had two collaborating researchers! Research design and method were easily established over our first tasting and then very serious data collection and analysis ensued. Sometimes we had to collect multiple samples in a day to ensure fair comparison without the risk of forgetting intricate details. Fortunately there were no ethical considerations involved in this research as we conducted the testing on ourselves. Our results are as follows: the best hazelnut was at Cremeria Cavour, the best pistacchio was at Cremeria Santo Stefano and the best unique flavour, cherry with chocolate and hazelnut pieces, was at Cremeria La Vecchia Stalla. The surprise result was that each one of these flavours is from a different cremeria and there isn’t ONE best cremeria. The conclusion is that when in Bologna, enjoying the best gelato involves a combination of consumption and exercise - one flavour at each location!

Laurie is very serious about her studies! All that gelato feeds brain activity.

One of the seven secrets of Bologna, The Whispering Gallery has a cool physics trick. If you stand in one corner and whisper, someone in the diagonal opposite corner can hear you clearly. When this vaulted portico was built in the 13th century it was a surprise to discover this unintended acoustic effect. It served the lepers of the time well as they could do confession and not put the priests at risk.

Neptune’s Secret Finger is another of Bologna’s not so well kept secrets. At the right angle behind Neptune’s fountain in the Piazza Maggiore, Neptune’s outstretched thumb looks like… Was this a cheeky bit of Renaissance humour or a surprising find by a tourist who happened to look that way when walking past?

Back during the second century BC Roman Empjre, Bologna was called Bononia and was conveniently located along the route between Rimini (on the coast) and Piacenza. As with much of that era, old roads and buldings were covered with new ones as society and cities modernized. When doing some renovations along a retail stretch in the early 2000s, the road was discovered under the site of what is now a Roche Bobois store. The city decided that if the store wanted to keep this location, it had to provide access to anyone who wanted to see it, regardless of their shopping intent or lack thereof. So here it is, the part of the ancient Roman road network that connected the empire, in the basement of Roche Bobois!

The canale dele Moline is part of the old network of canals in Bologna and is at the end of our street (already sounding like Bologna is home away from home!). These were built during the 12th-16th centuries for agriculture, sanitation and to move goods, especially from the silk mills (moline means mills). In medieval times, Bologna was a major silk production hub with over 100 water mills powered by these canals. There is just a bit of the original 60km network of canals still visible. One day I walked past this one and it was completely drained! Turns out they do this occasionally for repairs, maintenance and cleaning (I’m sure they can get pretty stinky).

A very apt name for a tattoo parlour.

The longest portico in Bologna, and the world, is the Portico di San Luca. Built between 1674-1793 to protect religious pilgrimages to the San Luca Sanctuary, this special place is 3.8km long with 666 arches (a dodgy number if you ask me for a path leading to a Catholic basilica) and 489 steps up. No wonder it’s now used for a workout, in addition to the odd pilgrimage!

We took a three-day pasta-making class before we left Bologna at Vecchia Scuola Bologna (VSB). This was serious stuff! The owner of the school, Alessandra Spisni, learned the art of Sfoglia (traditional hand rolled pasta) as a child at her grandmothers knees. She wanted to be an astronaut but the kitchen calling was louder. She started the schoolIn 1993 in an effort to preserve this traditional art of pasta-making and is now a famous television personality! Her methods are magical and we have to hurry home and order the necessary equipment (a large beech wood board and a beech matarello, the special rolling pin) so we don’t forget how! Like ballet, there a first position, second position and third position in the rolling.

Here we are making Strozzapreti (literally “strangle the priests”). This regular occurrence got a bit old after a while so the women had a great idea. They created this and when the priest came by at lunchtime, they were offered strozzapreti, which they politely declined!

Bologna Road Trip #1

All work and no play…well, there was a lot of play mixed in with the work. Call it working play, as we had to practice our Italian skills as we road tripped around!

Our first chariot took us to small towns around Bologna. It was twice the price to rent in Bologna so we hopped on a train to Ferrara to rent the car. Of course this was when we were operating in blissful ignorance about the International Driving License rule.

First stop: San Giovanni in Persiceto. This little town near Bologna is very much a locals place but Piazzetta degli Inganni is what attracts the occasional visitor. Back in the early 1980s, in an attempt to bring this little square back to life, the town enticed the Italian film set designer Gino Pellegrini to paint murals all around the square for its summer film festival. Well, this fabulous artwork lasted long beyond the summer festival and remains the big draw for this little town.

As with everywhere in Italy, there are many places to stop for a “chiacchierata”. When you listen in, everyone sounds like they’re old friends catching up, sharing the latest gossip, regardless of which generation they belong to. Young and old are connected.

Dozza is a beautiful hilltop village just outside Bologna. Its claim to fame is murals that decorate the houses and shops of the two streets that make up the village. This was no happenstance revitalization of a dwindling medieval village. In 1960, watching the younger generation leave in search of action and modernity, and historic buildings decline through lack of interest, designer/art promoter Dino Gavina and artist/art critic Antonio Rinaldi hatched a plan! Bring art out of the museums to make it more accessible, blend modern with the old and take art beyond decoration and into the cityscape. These two visionaries invited contemporary artists to come paint directly on the exterior walls of the buildings. It was such a success that it turned into a biennial event, now heading into its 29th round with some 200 murals on the walls, staircases and various nooks and crannies in the village. It was a win-win: historic buildings were preserved in a creative way; art became more accessible to everyone; and tourism increased, giving a reason for the youth to stay and build a life here.

Bologna Road Trip #2

Our second road trip from Bologna took us up north to Piemonte for some wine tasting. As always, there were surprises along the way!

We had to stop in Parma on the way because, well, Prosciutto and Parmigiano Reggiano of course! But we discovered this town is actually much more than its cheese and ham claim to fame.

The big question is can Leo fix the world!

The Parma cathedral is a Romanesque masterpiece dating back to the 11th century. It took nearly 100 years to build but there were lots of updates and additions to the interior along the way. The most famous part of this cathedral was created by Correggio in the 1520s. It depicts the Assumption of the Virgin Mary, not to confused with the ascension of her well known son. Jesus ascended to heaven by his own divine power. Mary didn’t have such power so she was “called” to heaven by God. She was Jesus’ mom after all. Elevated by ain a swirling vortex of angels and clouds this artwork is considered to be one of the first truly “3D” illusionistic domes. Mary is to the left of the dark dot near the centre of the dome, surrounded by angels and the regular cast of biblical characters. She’s the biggest figure in a pale pink robe with a blue mantle gazing up toward the figure in the middle of it all, Jesus. He’s ready to receive his mother into her heavenly home…hope he cleaned up before she got there.

While Correggio’s dome is the main draw in the cathedral, the classic biblical stories on the side aisles by Lattanzio Gambara draw the art students.

Like Parma’s cathedral, its baptistery is also considered to be one of the most important medieval monuments in Italy. Started in 1196, it’s the place of Christian rebirth and eternity. Get baptized and you’ve got your golden ticket to salvation. Full of amazing frescoes and reliefs depicting the life of Christ and his band of merry men, it would be an inspiration to all who got dipped in the baptismal font. Interestingly, around the bottom are zodiac reliefs showing the trials of every month of the year, marking time and seasons. You’d think that the zodiac would lean a little to paganism and superstition and would have no place in a Christian space devoted to an unyielding belief in the power of God but not so! The stars, sun and moon were all part of God’s celestial clockwork. They were not about fate but rather God’s instructions written in the sky.

There is so much to be learned in a cheese shop where the staff live and breathe their product. Parma is the land of Parmigiano Regianno and prosciutto di Parma. First clue is in the name. I knew that Parmigiano Reggiano is aged and the price varies based on the amount of time spent aging. What I didn’t know if there is a seasonality to buying the best cheese. Right now, as we launch into summer, the best Parmigiano Reggiano to eat is the one aged 24 months. Why? You’d think the older cheese would be the one recommended. But no! The cows milked 24 months ago were eating the freshest, green grass which results in the best tasting milk. So in summer, buy the 24-month aged cheese. In the winter, buy the 30-month aged cheese, because that would have been produced 2.5 years ago with the summer milk. We bought some cheese to eat now and some more to take to our next destination. The cheesemonger switched from Italian to English to make sure we understood exactly how he was wrapping the cheese and how we needed to rewrap it once we opened it. And where in the fridge we should store it (the bottom shelf just above the vegetable drawer). That’s passion.

Bubbling Bologna

Coming from sleepy Sicily we took the overnight train to Bologna. Of course Adam totally geeked out over how they got all the train cars onto the ferry at 1am (when we should have been sleeping). Pretty fascinating actually.

Bologna is a hip, fashionable, youthful, foodie city with the world’s oldest Western university, founded before the close of the 11th century. Oh, and there’s food there too! Bologna is the seat of ragù (aka bolognese sauce), lasagna, tagliatelle and tortellini, in all of which the locals take great pride. It’s no wonder it’s nicknames are La Dotta (the learned, for its academia), La Rossa (the red, for its rooftops) and La Grassa (the fat, for its devotion to food).

Bologna is known for its extensive network of porticoes, 62km in the city, 42km of which are crammed into the historic city centre. Some stunningly beautiful and others merely functional, they started being built in the Middle Ages to extend living spaces. The growth of the university probably had a lot to do with the accommodation pressure. Sound familiar? Maybe some Canadian university towns could take a page out of Bologna’s book!

Hanging out in a portico.

Somehow we caught wind of an ice cream shop in Bologna that was supposed to be the best. Well, every gelateria lays claim to being the best, I’m sure, however Cremeria Cavour is hands down the winner. Seriously worth the trip to Bologna just for the gelato. The best gelato we’ve EVER had and that is saying something. Creamy like you’ve never had before. Classic and unusual flavours that will entice you back the next day so you can try something different. I admit that this was our lunch three out of four days in Bologna. The third lunch we went traditional (full disclosure: it was Easter Monday and the gelato shop was closed) and had lasagna and tagliatele with ragù. Excellent. Bologna deserves the title.

The enormous and imposing Bologna Cathedral (San Pietro) had some of the most interesting stories, features and quirks. The facade is the first point of note. It’s unfinished. The bottom half is finished with elegant pink and white marble. The top half is uneven brick (this uneven brick is part of the design to accommodate the marble facade). Turns out they ran out of money during construction so some details were dropped. Personally, I would have pushed for curb appeal and cut some corners inside… that’s not the way they build cathedrals though. Kind of like landscaping when you build a house. If you run out of money, it’s the landscaping that suffers. This basilica has no transcept. As with many of these old churches, they expanded over time. This one was heading towards being larger that St Peter’s in Rome. The Pope was not about to be upstaged so he ordered the construction of the Archiginnasio, the first seat of the university, adjacent to the church, thereby using up the space where the transcept would have been. Needless to say, the basilica did not end up bigger that the Pope’s home turf.

I think itAnother quirk outside is the police presence. Turns out it’s due to something inside that got some people’s knickers in a knot…

…so inside the cathedral, wealthy patrons helped fund the construction but, of course, needed something in return for their contributions. Twenty-two chapels complete with naming opportunities and personal decoration did the trick. One chapel in particular, the Capella Bolognini, is still talked about to this day. Its rather original painting by by Giovanni da Modena of Heaven and Hell, inspired by Dante’s Divine Comedy, is a sight to behold. Zoom in and look at the details. Sorry, it was hard to get a good angle for the photo as the space in front was roped off, so I’ve picked one off the Internet. The monster in the middle is eating people through its mouth. Now look down between its legs to see the unfortunate folk on their exit. Now here’s why the police are stationed outside: In 2002 and 2006, Islamic terrorists were thwarted in their efforts to blow up the basilica. Why? Because this fresco was an insult to Islam as there is a depiction of Mohammed being devoured by demons. And if there’s any question as to whether it’s really Mohammed, his name has been written under his image. You’ll find Mohammed’s foot dangling over the central demon’s head.

Bologna was strategically located here between the Reno and Savena rivers. It’s always about being near water for food, trade and manufacturing. The rivers were gradually covered over thanks to urban sprawl but some hidden bits can still be seen. This view, through a peep through window where people line up for their turn to see. We didn’t line up behind the 50 or so people we saw on our way to dinner. But after dinner we there were just five people so we waited our turn.

In homage to the abundance of water, this fountain in honour of Poseidon sits at the entrance to the Piazza Maggiore. Water comes from everywhere, including the breasts of the four not so graceful sea nymphs at the base of the fountain. Apparently this fountain is still fed by the water from the now underground rivers.

At their peak (12-13th centuries), there were 180 towers in Bologna, built by the wealthy to show off their power but also served as a means of defence. This is the moon rising up beside the Asinelli Tower, the city’s tallest at 97 metres.

The Pietre di Paragone are unnoticed markings in squares in Italy to show the dimensions of local measurements to avoid any confusion in trade. These were used until 1861 when Italy adopted the metric system.

Piazza della Mercanzia is the Chamber of Commerce. Note the white balcony in the centre. This is where the sentences of merchants accused of dodgy business would be read out. If the merchants were found to be guilty of bankruptcy, they would be tied to a stake in the front of the building and subjected to public ridicule.
