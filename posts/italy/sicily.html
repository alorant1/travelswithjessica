Sicily, Italy

Palermo

Segesta and the Valle dei Templi

It’s time for the Ancients…we left Palermo for our road trip across Sicily. Sicily was invasion central from the beginning. There was action even before the Greeks came in 750 BC, followed by the Romans in 200 BC, not to mention the Byzantines, Arabs and Normans after the Common Era. The “little island that could” was strategically located not only in terms of trade routes in the Mediterranean and protection from the Arabs, Carthaginians and other marauders, but also for its growing conditions. The soil here still produces amazing food (more on that another day!). We traced a bit of this history of invasions in Segesta and Agrigento where the Valley of the Temples is found. Amazing what the archeologists can piece together from fragments to create a story. And like with any science, it’s based on the best interpretation of the information available. Then new information pops up and that brings new theories.

Villa Romana del Casale

Ragusa

And now for something near and dear to our hearts: Food porn in Ragusa. You asked for it Lesley Bentley! We stayed in the most wonderful hotel and our first dinner at Ciano definitely set the bar high. Luca Gulino is a master of his craft. At 31, this chef is definitely one to watch. And such a kind and personable man. I’m sure we will be hearing more and more about him in the culinary realm. Every dish was like a present. Layers of delicious ingredients artfully wrapped around gifts of flavour.

Ragusa

Thanks to the massive earthquake that destroyed half of southeastern Sicily in 1693, the region has become the crown jewel of baroque architecture, more specifically the more flamboyant Sicilian Baroque. Of the eight towns that were rebuilt and are now part of the Unesco world heritage list, we managed to visit five as we were roaming around the area.

Sweet Siracusa

What a lovely town. There’s a definite vibe here. Lots more action in this town. It’s almost like they acknowledge they have lots of history and ruins of a time long gone by, but it doesn’t define them. Much has not been rebuilt, rather fenced off and left as found. Was it so destroyed from skirmishes and earthquakes that the archeologists couldn’t figure out the puzzle? Were the archeologists spent after figuring out Segesta, Selinunte and Agrigento? Or did they just decide they wouldn’t be defined by the rubble?

Terrific Taormina﻿

Explosive Etna and terrific Taormina. We had low expectations of Taormina, assuming it would be tourist trap central. Well, it is but it has an elegance, charm and beauty that make up for it. And neighbouring Mount Etna! What a fantastic opportunity to experience an active volcano, thankfully in sleeping mode when we went up. This wraps our time in Sicily. We said goodbye to Miss D (our so very cute red Fiat 500) and are now on an overnight train to the mainland. Adam totally geeked out on how they got all the train cars onto the ferry!

Lo Zingaro

Spectacular, sunny Sicily! An amazing first day of my weekend getaway with my wonderful cousin Joanna Miller! A stunning hike through Lo Zingaro Nature Reseve from Scopello to San Vito Lo Capo. Hot day with a couple of dips in icy water (but not quite as icy as Lapland…). Capped of the day with a trip up to the medieval hilltop town of Erice for sunset and dinner. If not for dinner still going strong outside our window (it’s midnight), I’d be fast asleep…ah Italy

Marsala

After dodging being stranded on the island of Favignana (so windy they couldn’t guarantee the ferry would come back to Trapani that day, we elected to go to visit the Trapani salt pans and Marsala. Who knew the effort that goes into the production of the humble salt! Marsala was quiet on a Sunday afternoon but the Pellegrino Winery happened to be open (for a giant Baptism Party) so we managed to get a tour and Marsala tasting.

Tempio Greco di Segesta

Location, location, location! Perched on a hill in Segesta is this Doric temple built around 430 BC. Wild and overgrown with grasses and spring flowers, you can just imagine lovely nymphs making flower crowns and frolicking in nature. It’s one of the most well-preserved temples, virtually intact, except for the complete absence of a roof. Some believe the town was attacked and permanently interrupted the completion. Nearby, on top of Monte Barbaro, is a semi circular theatre. They say it seats 4,000 people. I figure those people didn’t have the same personal space issues we have nowadays!

Palermo

First day in Palermo and what a fun-filled adventure! Food, culture, history here, there and everywhere!

Cefalu

Cefalù is about 45 minutes on the train from Palermo; if you’re ever in Palermo, you really should carve out a day to go. Gorgeous seaside town with a UNESCO World Heritage cathedral, ruins dating back to the 4th century BC, amazing views, fantastic food and a lovely beach…

Monreale

Last day in Palermo. A pious, creepy, classy and food-filled day to wrap things up in this cool city with a definite edge. No photos allowed in the creepy part of the day, a visit to the Catacombe dei Cappuccini. Originally (1599) the monastery’s place for embalmed friars, the city’s wealthy paid to have their dead embalmed and placed in the catacombs, often wearing clothes if their own choosing. Families would come to visit their loved ones and keep the bodies in presentable condition. What did we see? Tons of skeletons; babies, children, adults, men, women, monks…all in various degrees of decomposition. Quite a sight, that’s for sure.

Scala dei Turchi

Road trip! We wound our way down highways and through the countryside first to Selinunte, an Ancient Greek city from the 5th century BC, then to Scala dei Turchi, limestone “stairs” that drop into the sea.

Valle Dei Templi Agrigento

There’s something about ancient stones piled up on top of each other … Built in and around the 5th and 6th centuries BC, there’s something for everyone here: temples in honour of gods and goddesses, private homes along streets, great views, grand entrance gates and annual parties. Here’s my favourite theme party of theirs: when Hades fell in love with Demeter’s daughter Persephone and whisked her away to the underworld, Demeter was so despondent that the landscape and agriculture turned brown and died. She cut a deal with Hades to let Persephone come back spring and summer and that’s why these months are our growing season. Here’s where it gets fun. In Agrigento they had an annual three day festival to honour Demeter (and mothers in general). Female pigs were sacrificed and their blood was collected to enrich the soil. Cakes in the shape of genitalia were baked and eaten by the women. Unclear which genitalia were the most popular… We ended the day in the lovely town of Piazza Armerina with much more “modern” buildings.

Piazza Armerina

After leaving Piazza Armerina, we landed in Villa Romana del Casale. Much more modern than where we’ve been so far, the Villa Romana del Casale was founded early in the 4th century (AD). It has the most impressive collection of Roman mosaics in the world. Oh, and like so many places we’ve been and will go, it’s a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Something about Sicily…

Palazzolo Acreide

And the biking begins! First day riding from Palazzolo Acreide to Modica with a coffee stop in Giarratana (meet our new friend Andrea), and lunch in Ragusa Ilba. Spectacular day with great women, stunning scenery, lovely towns good food and perfect coffee.

Modica

And the biking begins! First day riding from Palazzolo Acreide to Modica with a coffee stop in Giarratana (meet our new friend Andrea), and lunch in Ragusa Ilba. Spectacular day with great women, stunning scenery, lovely towns good food and perfect coffee.

Marzamemi

Visit Marzamemi, a little fishing village on the Ionian Sea. You will love it!

Noto

Day 4 Marzamemi to Noto. This was a foodie day with a wine tasting lunch and capped off with a fabulous dinner in Noto at Manna. Noto is a beautiful Baroque town.

Noto Antica

Day 5. Noto out and back again, through the Riserva Naturale Cavagrande Del Cassibile, past some Necropoli and a pop in to Noto Antica. Need I mention the well deserved gelato and Prosecco?

Siracusa

Day 6 and our last ride from Noto to Siracusa along the coast. What an amazing trip this has been with a remarkable group of women. Thank you to Ciclofree for organizing the tour with so many special touches. If you want to cycle in Sicily, I would highly recommend Ciclofree. Ciao bellas. Can’t wait till next year!

Pantalica

We said farewell to most of our group this morning and stayed on to hike among the Necropoli di Pantalica near Sortino. Here there are upwards of 4,000 tombs cut into the limestone gorge above the Anapo river. Dating from the 13th to the 7th century BC, these tombs would have been sealed with stones or large slabs once the dead were placed inside (with food and water for their journey into the afterlife). How they cut so many tombs on the flat rock face and how they got the bodies in and sealed them up is remarkable.

Catania

Last night before heading home. First impression of Catania was not great…maybe because we are staying in a bit of a dodgy area. But explore we did in our limited time and found the coolest spot! The San Berillo District has seen everything. After the famous 1693 earthquake, this neighbourhood was heading in a very noble direction with its baroque buildings. But enter Fascist buildings and destruction from the war and we are left with a haven of narrow streets, buildings in different styles and various states of disrepair. All it takes is the creativity of artists and hipsters to turn it into a super cool district oozing with charm. However, if you have no reason to be in Catania, I would skip it…San Berillo is cool, but not worth going to Catania just for that.
