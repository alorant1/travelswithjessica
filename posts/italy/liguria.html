Legendary Liguria

It’s off season on the coast so the villages were pretty quiet on a weekday, some so quiet that most cafés, restaurants and shops were closed. Our first stop heading west was the medieval village of Noli whose 11th century fortress, perched on top of Mount Ursino, was an important defence against invaders and pirates who made the Ligurian Coast a regular pit stop.

Snaking our way up the footpath past olive trees, churches and houses, I spied some ripe figs! Don’t worry, I was safe and scored two beauties!

These curving, cobbled streets seem to stretch to magical places beckoning us. Thankfully these old buildings have these struts to keep them from falling in on each other.

Noli, from the Castello at the top of the hill. For a small place, it had 72 towers and there are a few that remain. It must have been amazing to see 72 towers crammed into this town! Not only was Noli well-positioned to defend itself against invaders, it was also run by very strategically-minded people. It was a wealthy finance hub and in 1192 it had the foresight to buy its freedom and independence from the Marquis Henry Il Del Carretto for a mere 1720 Genoa Lira. Although relatively tiny, Noli managed to maintain its independence for centuries and even had its own fleet and currency. They deftly aligned themselves with their mightier cousin Genoa which proved to be quite a symbiotic relationship. An ally and port access in exchange for protection and preserving autonomy. An Italian version of NATO long ago.

The empty beach in Finale Ligure where we stopped for lunch at the only open restaurant we could find. Lucky for us the food was fabulous!

On to Cervo, my favourite of the day. And I can sure pick them as it turns out Cervo is recognized as one of “I Borghi più belli d’Italia” (The Most Beautiful Villages of Italy).

Cervo’s roots are medieval and you can see it in the narrow, winding streets and houses that are still here and in use.

Beautiful late afternoon view from the 17th century Church of San Giovanni Battista. It must have been hard to stay inside the church when you know there’s this view outside. Its nickname is the Church of the Corallini because it was financed by local coral fishermen. Sadly, they didn’t know the environmental damage they were doing to their coastline.

A number of Cervo’s little alleys and pathways have been dressed up. This place is quite a haven for artists of all kinds who have helped to restore buildings and park their workshops here. Another disadvantage of off-season is none of those ateliers were open.

The flip side of it being off-season is we had the place virtually to ourselves!

You know real people live here when the laundry is hanging out the windows.

The start of the sunset peeking through Cervo.

Once we got as far as Cervo we were almost at the French border so we went to Nice for dinner!

Our next coastal day trip was east towards Portofino. Being a weekend it was definitely busier, which gave the day a more upbeat vibe! We started with coffee in Boccadasse, about 20 minutes outside of Genoa. Apparently this is weekend strolling territory for the Genoese. The strolling was pretty much roadside so we moved on after we realized there wasn’t much more to enjoy other than this view back to the old town hugging the bay. Thinking about it now, I bet the prime strolling might well have been the other side of the bay in the old part of town.

Now here’s the strolling spot! Passeggiata Anita Garibaldi runs a couple of kilometres along the edge of the cliff in Nervi. In case you are wondering, Anita was the wife of our Italian Unification hero Guiseppe. She too was a freedom fighter and I’m sure quite a powerful woman in her day.

The views along the cliff side path were stunning…so was the summer weather in November!

My favourite town in this direction was Camogli where we stopped for lunch. A fishing village for centuries, we had fabulous seafood by the boats…

…accompanied by a glass of wine!

Camogli is known for its colourful seafront buildings. This is not just to be picturesque, it actually has a practical purpose. It helped the fishermen identify their homes from the sea. I’m not sure what else was happening on the boats that made it difficult for the guys to find their way home…

This young fellow is carrying on Camogli’s centuries-old fishing tradition. He pulled out these tiny fish within seconds of dropping in his line. He put them in a plastic grocery bag and went on repeat. The bag was flipping around beside him and it wasn’t because of the wind.

And to finish the day, we found our way to the infamous Portofino. We had to go see what all the fuss is about. It was the end of the day and most things were closed except for the excellent gelato shop! My hazelnut gelato paired well with my pear and chocolate sorbetto.

Portofino’s beautiful coastline. It’s worth the hype but I’m glad we were there at the end of the day when most people had cleared out. It almost felt like we had it to ourselves. No super-yachts in residence today.

We hiked up a path from the harbour to the lighthouse in time to catch the glorious sunset before we leave Italy. Ciao for now bella Italia!
