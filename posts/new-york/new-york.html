From the Lenape tribe’s seasonal hunting and fishing camp when America was first colonized, to Pier 54, and now Little Island, this spot on the Hudson River seems to have always been a gathering place. Pier 54 was the British Cunard-White Star line’s port of departure and return for trans-Atlantic voyages between 1910 and 1935 and it famously received the survivors of the Titanic in 1912. After falling into disuse in the 70s and 80s, the area was revived by the LGBTQ community and became a part of annual pride festivities and summer events. Hurricane Sandy put an end to all that in 2012, damaging this and other piers in the area. Nine years later, Little Island has injected new life into this place steeped in history as a fabulous new community park with performance spaces, ever-changing landscaping and stunning views.

Testimonial Tenement Museum

Harris managed to save $50 (about five months of rent) to buy a sewing machine and started a garment factory in the family’s living room. The five kids who slept on mats on the floor had to be up and out early to make room for the business to churn out dresses for department stores. Harris created 130 dresses per month and, after paying his four workers (baster, sewer, finisher, presser), made $0.15 per dress and cleared $10 per month after rent and other expenses. Jenny handled the family budget, using that $10 to feed and clothe their family of seven.

There was no running water or electricity in the building so Jenny would have to go down to the courtyard to draw water from a single pump in the courtyard. That’s also where the four privies were for 120 people to share, as well as the one coal storage space. Lining up for water, coal and to use the toilet is where women built community, where they were mentored by those who came before them, where they learned to manage life in this new country.

Hinde Amhanitski published the first Yiddish cookbook in the US, an invaluable resource for women trying to figure out how to cook and bake without familiar ingredients. She promised her readers that if they followed her recipes, their children would not suffer stomach aches and other food related illnesses.

Being observant Jews, they kept kosher. And although kosher beef was twice as expensive as non-kosher beef (12 cents/pound vs 6 cents) they didn’t begrudge the price because they had no choice. Keeping kosher was their way of life. But in 1902, the American Beef Trust which held a monopoly on kosher beef, raised the prices by 50% overnight, to 18 cents/pound. Don’t mess with women trying to feed their families! The 1902 Kosher Beef Boycott was born. There were 124 butchers in this one square mile neighbourhood and, being part of the community, some butchers boycotted the Trust themselves. This little rebellion only lasted four days. Their hands were tied; they had businesses to run and families to feed.

The customers (the women) who were neighbours and friends, felt betrayed and the community not only picked up the boycott, but picked up radical protest methods from what was happening in the city around them. This was not what you would expect from middle-aged Jewish women! 3,000 women took to the streets to right a very big wrong. They ran a door knocking campaign and entered their neighbours homes, searching for beef. If they found any, they would set it on fire and throw it out the window to the street. Their protest’s tagline was the Trust was stealing the meat off the bones of your children. Who could argue with that? They threw bricks through butcher’s windows. They went to the synagogue and interrupted services, stood up in balconies and begged the rabbis to support the cause. Some even stormed the bima (pulpit). This was a huge deal as women had no rights in the synagogue, being relegated to the balconies, and certainly were not allowed to set foot on the sacred pulpit. They gained the support of rabbis and the local Yiddish newspapers which helped further their cause.

They also gained the attention of the police and after three weeks of these disturbances, 85 people were arrested (3/4 of which were women) and stood trial at the Essex Market court house. Support for the cause spread to other cities with large Jewish populations, even Chicago, the home of the Beef Trust. In June 1902 the Beef Trust decided they had lost enough money and dropped kosher beef prices to 14 cents per pound. The predominantly uneducated and illiterate women of the Lower East Side tenements were successful in righting that very big wrong!

Bustling Brooklyn

We spent a fabulous final day in Brooklyn with friends who took us on a 30km bike ride through parts of Brooklyn that felt like completely different worlds, to Battery Park City, and back to Brooklyn over its namesake bridge. Rain was strategically falling while we were on the ferry and while we stopped for a snack. Perfect planning! Perfect Day!

Our route. Yup, on a paper map! There’s nothing better than riding through a city and its neighbourhoods to get a sense of the real world. And what a variety of worlds we passed through!

First stop was the Brooklyn Museum. This sculpture by Deborah Kaas out front embodies two big worlds of Brooklyn!

This little girl had such fun running among the steps in front of the museum’s musical fountain.

In keeping with the art vibe, we rode over to the Pratt Institute and strolled through the gardens full of sculptures. I think Adam found some new office space!

A fire truck, siren blaring, is desperately trying to get down Bedford Ave in Brooklyn at 1pm on a Friday. The Friday afternoon pre-Shabbat traffic thwarted its efforts. Countless School buses racing to get kids home for Shabbat, cars, delivery vehicles, bicycles, all getting in its way (and not OUT of its way) at every intersection. The siren slows to a sad wail. On our bikes, we past the fire truck several times until it was so far behind we could no longer see its flashing lights. Despite the siren, I really hope it had nowhere important to go.

Two birds keeping watch over the UN across the East River.

The ferry took us from Brooklyn’s Hunters Point across the East River to Wall St. Such a great perspective from the water.

A beautiful redevelopment of the Domino Sugar factory, a place with a long and sordid past. Opened in Williamsburg in 1856, this place employed up to 4,500 people at its peak. Not really something to be proud of these days; the sugar was harvested by slaves, mixed into a dirty slurry, boiled in enormous vats and filtered through charred animal bones. If that doesn’t turn you off sweets then I don’t know what would! The mixture was “whipped, beaten, flayed, hurled into ‘grain,’” The Illustrated American magazine reported in 1894. “The process is very wild and terrible, like a caged cyclone.” The working conditions were brutal, churning out 1,500 tons of sugar per day. That’s a serious sweet tooth.

One World Trade Center, the North Tower, was the first building struck by terrorists on Sept 11, 2001. Carrying the same name, the new One World Trade Center (the narrow building with the spire) is the tallest building in the Western Hemisphere, and the seventh-tallest in the world. Its height in feet (1776) is an intentional nod to the year the US Declaration of Independence was signed. It’s quite poetic that a few days before the 9/11 anniversary, the clouds came together in a halo above this tower built on Ground Zero.

Layering of bridges.

Ellis Island, where countless immigrants arrived, including some of my family back in the late 1800s.

The American Merchant Mariners Memorial was designed based on a photograph. American merchant marine ships were pressed into service during WWII, shipping troops and supplies across dangerous waters. A Nazi U-boat attacked a merchant marine ship and while its mariners clung to the remnants of their ship, the Nazis took a photo of their victims that inspired the sculptor Marisol Escobar. More than 9,000 merchant marines were killed in the war, the highest casualty rate of any service branch in WWII.
