Heavenly Ha Long Bay

As legend would have it, the gods sent a family of dragons to protect the newly established Vietnam against invaders. The dragons spit out jewels and jade which transformed into the islands and islets in Ha Long Bay, linked together to form a defensive perimeter. As invaders approached in their boats, giant rocks magically appeared just in time for the invaders to crash into them. The Dragons decided to settle in the now peaceful area. Ha Long means descending dragon (where the mother dragon settled) and Bai Tu Long means children attend upon (where the dragon children settled to attend to their mother).

The geologists might disagree with how this region was formed. They dig a bit deeper back in time, starting some 500 million years ago when this area that was all part of the ocean, got hit with some tectonic movement and spent the next 100 million years forming limestone mountains. Over the last 20 million years erosion, thanks to the tides and the limestone, have created the stunning landscape of 2000 islands and who knows how many caves and water tunnels we have the privilege of navigating today.

Bing Vieng fishing village dates from the 19th century. Surrounded almost completely by small islands, it started out as an anchorage for boats and refuge from storms. In time, people started building houses (on docks or boats tethered together) there and the community has grown to 80 homes. About 30 children from the floating fishing village go to a mainland boarding school, paid for by an education charity. The parents are motivated to send their kids to school as they themselves have no education. If this seems like a great place to retire or resettle, you’re in for a big commitment. There is no “immigration” to this village unless by marriage.

Pearls Glorious Pearls

Of course we had to learn about pearls while in Ha Long Bay! 1893 was a magic year in the pearl world. That’s when Kokichi Mikimoto figured out how to produce a cultured pearl. A hundred years later the Vietnamese learned how to implant pearls into oysters and grow them in Ha Long Bay. A booming business now exists!

Making a cultured pearl is a little like in vitro fertilization. The mantle strip is removed from an oyster and cut into small pieces. A receiving oyster shell is pried open and a small piece of donor mantle tissue (the “saibo”) is placed side by side with a spherical bead of oyster shell (it looks like a tiny pearl) in the reproductive organ of the receiving oyster. The shell is then closed and attached to a rack or a string and placed back in the sea. The donor mantle forms a pearl sac around the bead and layers of pearl nacre coat the bead forming the pearl.

This shows the stages of the mantle preparation. On the right, in the shell, the mantle is along the edge of the flesh of the oyster. On the wooden board, you have the whole mantle piece on the right, cleaned up in the middle, and cut into the tiny pieces to be inserted into the host oyster on the left. In the container at the top of the photo are the “seeds” that get implanted with the mantle.
