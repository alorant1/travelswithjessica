Zihua is a true slice of heaven, especially after monumental Mexico City, the fifth largest city in the world, where nobody seems to agree on the population…let’s call it somewhere between 22 million (official stats) and 40 million (what the Uber driver told us, probably because he knows all the people the census couldn’t reach). Our amazing Flo made an extensive list of amazing things to do and we are enjoying every second!

What’s the first thing you do on arrival in Zihua? Don your swimsuit and get in the water!

Our home beach.

Thankfully the safety strap wasn’t attached to my ski accident ankle of last year! Back to the dog. We named him Brava because he was so brave. This little fella had a 6” gash in his back. We could see his flesh and a bunch of maggots crawling around (it was a three-day-old wound). Brava is a beach stray and everyone on the beach knows him and likes him…except, apparently, a waiter at a neighbouring restaurant who scared him off with a swing of a machete. Dana stepped in to solve the problem. She rallied the troops to find a vet, get him to come to assess the situation and do what needed to be done. The lovely vet came, sedated Brava, cleaned and stitched him up and one of the restaurant staff took him home to recuperate. A lovely dog has been saved (and maybe adopted by the waiter?!). I don’t want to know what happens to the machete wielding waiter if they ever figure out who it was!

Zihuantanejo is known as an eco-friendly community. One of the very special things that happens down here is protecting turtles. The mama turtles show up on the beach, each laying 120 or so eggs. When early morning walkers notice the actual laying or the disturbed sand indicating a nest, the “turtle authorities” are notified. The eggs are carefully removed and re-nested in this net-covered space and mark the laying date on sticks.

When the turtles are ready to hatch, they put them in a bin and take them out of their nesting home to the beach. They wait for the sun to almost set before they tip over the bin and let them run to the water. Today they released 99 babies. Knowing that one or two in a thousand will survive and come back to this beach to lay their own eggs does not bode well for these 99 cuties. However a few days ago, they released a whopping 2750 so Zihuantanejo can expect a few returns in a few years.
