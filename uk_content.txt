This was a walled city so as the population grew, they were forced to build up, sometimes reaching 10-12 storeys. The rich lived among the poor but they got the breezy top floors with the poor relegated to the damp, dark, lower levels. This made old Edinburgh one of the most densely populated places in 17th century Europe. The advantage of these socially-mixed residential areas was if the poor had a problem, so did the rich so issues were more likely to be addressed. A Scottish mile was 1/8th (220 yards) longer than the English Mile. Edinburgh's Royal Mile is 24 yards short of the Scottish mile. The length was coincidental but it made for a good name!]

The church was nicknamed for the "tron", a public weighing beam where merchants arriving in Edinburgh to sell their goods like grain, butter, coal, salt and meat had to have them officially weighed for market. What happened to Charles? He was the first king to be charged with treason against his own people and was ultimately beheaded in 1649, thanks to his pushing religion and royal power on the people. The iron cage on the right hand side of the photo is not a decorative element of the cemetery. In fact, it's a mortsafe. In the late 18th and early 19th centuries, Edinburgh was a world centre for medical training. Students needed fresh, dead bodies for dissection. At the time, according to the law, the med school only had access to the bodies of those executed. But with only a handful of executions per year, demand far exceeded supply. [TEXT: The Falkirk Wheel is an engineer-at-heart husband's paradise. Nestled about 45 minutes from Edinburgh sits this remarkable marvel. Here's the backstory. Scotland has two major canals: the Union Canal which links Edinburgh and Falkirk; and the Forth & Clyde Canal which crosses central Scotland from the Firth of Forth (east) to the Firth of Clyde (west). In the 19th century, they were linked by a series of 11 locks opening up water transport between Edinburgh and Glasgow. Modern transportation like the railway and road network improved and left the canal transportation network in the dust. In 1933, the locks were dismantled and the two canals were cut off from each other. In the 1990s, the Millennium Link Project undertook the huge task of restoring and reconnecting Scotland's canals for leisure and tourism. The challenge to reconnect the Union Canal with the Forth & Clyde Canal without rebuilding the old staircase of locks to manage the 35m difference in height was solved by the Falkirk Wheel, which opened in 2002.]
[VIDEO: https://videos.files.wordpress.com/Mxl72I8G/falkirk-wheel.mov]
[TEXT: The Falkirk Wheel was the brainchild of architect Tony Kettle. £78 million later, Falkirk became home to world's one and only rotating boat lift. Each of the two huge gondolas (also called caissons) are 25m x 8m and can carry up to four boats each, depending on their size, of court. Part of the magic of these 500 litre gondolas is rooted in Archimedes' principle which shows that an object placed in a fluid displaces its own weight of that fluid. So the two gondolas' weight of 500 tonnes never changes, regardless of how many boats may be in them. The gondolas are always in perfect balance even if one has no boats and the other is full. And because of this perfect balance, the wheel only requires the energy equivalent to boil 8 kettles (of course that is the analogy, we are in the UK after all) to overcome the friction and get things moving. What once took a full day to navigate through the flight of 11 locks now takes less than 15 minutes from sailing in from once canal to sailing out into the other!]
